Gradient descent is the workhorse of modern optimization, but its behavior in non-convex landscapes is surprisingly rich. Here I explore a few trajectories to build intuition.

## The Setup

Consider the Rosenbrock function — a classic test case for optimization:

$$
f(x, y) = (1 - x)^2 + 100(y - x^2)^2
$$

Despite being smooth and low-dimensional, its narrow curved valley makes it notoriously difficult for vanilla gradient descent.

## Comparing Optimizers

Three optimizers on the same landscape:

1. **Vanilla SGD** oscillates heavily in the valley, taking many steps to converge.
2. **Momentum** smooths the path by accumulating velocity, cutting through the oscillations.
3. **Adam** adapts per-parameter step sizes, handling the anisotropic curvature naturally.

## Why This Matters

In higher dimensions, these differences amplify. The condition number of the Hessian — the ratio of largest to smallest eigenvalues — determines how ill-conditioned the problem is.

For a quadratic objective $\frac{1}{2}x^T H x$, gradient descent converges at a rate proportional to:

$$
\frac{\lambda_{\max} - \lambda_{\min}}{\lambda_{\max} + \lambda_{\min}}
$$

When this ratio is close to 1, convergence is fast. In deep learning, it's often close to 0, making vanilla SGD painfully slow without momentum or adaptive methods.

## Takeaway

The choice of optimizer is as much about geometry as it is about learning rate schedules. Visualizing these trajectories on low-dimensional test functions builds intuition that carries over to the high-dimensional problems we actually care about.
