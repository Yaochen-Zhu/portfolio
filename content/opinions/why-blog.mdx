Mathematical publishing is slow. A paper can take years from idea to print — and that's if everything goes smoothly. In between, there's a vacuum: notebooks full of observations, failed approaches, and partial results that never see the light of day.

## What we lose

When we only share finished work, we lose:

- **Negative results.** Knowing what *doesn't* work saves others time. A counterexample or a failed proof attempt is genuinely useful, but it rarely meets the bar for a paper.
- **Intuition.** The polished proof hides how the author really arrived at the result. A blog post can include the dead ends, the numerical experiments that suggested the conjecture, the heuristic reasoning.
- **Speed.** A blog post takes a day to write. A paper takes months. If you have an insight that helps someone else today, why wait?

## The GIF argument

Many mathematical ideas are inherently dynamic — a fixed point iteration converging, a flow on a manifold, a Monte Carlo sampler mixing. A static figure in a paper captures one frame; an animated GIF captures the process.

Making these visualizations is cheap. Hosting them on a personal site is also cheap. The barrier is only cultural.

## What I'm doing about it

This site is my answer. Posts here are not papers — they're explorations, notes, and visualizations that I think are worth sharing. Some may grow into papers eventually. Most won't. That's the point.
